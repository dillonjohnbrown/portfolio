Dillon J. Brown Contact Résumé

There are more music apps than ever. However, few of them are made for people like me. Instead of renting music from Spotify, I buy my own copy from sites like Bandcamp. Additionally, I have other kinds of audio files, like audiobooks, stand-up comedy albums, and longform podcasts no longer in a feed.

I want to make a suite of apps laser-focused on one medium each. These apps will share a codebase and similar interfaces, each fine-tuned to their content.

Every button follows Apple's Human Interface Guidelines and has a touch target of 44pt.

My ultra-wide scrubber is easy to use with one hand, by righties or lefties.

Scrubber controls on popular apps require far more accuracy to use.

I have trouble divining the meaning of the endless glyphs on my phone. Words communicate actions more clearly.

Grab the waveform from any location and slide horizontally to change your place in a song.

Color is sampled from the album artwork to tint the scrubber.

Upcoming chapters in your audiobook, or the next song in your queue, are shown inline.

Ads detected by chapter names are automatically skipped. You can also select which chapters you'd like to skip — neat, huh?

By default the UI has buttons. You can choose to remove them for a gesture-only interface.

Tap on the album artwork to play/pause. Swipe left and right to change tracks.

The shape around the bottom buttons is a clever detail to subtly hint at something more below.

 

This common design pattern has been used since the dawn of GUIs — tabs!

Buttons are obvious, and gestures are fast. I created this interface so both are first-class citizens.

You can swipe horizontally, or use the buttons, to change tracks.

Swipe vertically to scroll down and use the bottom tabs.

The scrubber and buttons stay on screen.

Swipe to quickly move between tabs.
